# Review[^1]

The book presents the 4th [[Paradigms of Scientific Discovery]], which is the data-intensive computing, where data-intensive science consists of 3 components:
	1) Capture
	2) Curation
	3) Analysis

![[Pasted image 20260405163412.png]]

He advocates for a unified database or Laboratory Information Management Systems (LIMS) that can store the data efficiently, while allowing querying, be it online on a cloud or offline, to the system irregardless of what kind of data type you have so that this method works for all disciplines of science. He gives interesting solutions to the problem of data capture/curation/analysis and how scientific discovery should ideally work in the modern age.

One problem with data delivery occurs when the database gets too large. At that point, instead of accessing the online data to your machine, it becomes more efficient to send your query online to the cloud for the server system to process your query and return back the results to you.

Most of the sections are very specific applications of data-intensive software and hardware, and don't hold as much value to an everyday person. I would recommend reading just Jim Gray's intro section or watching his lecture if it's available online since that's where the meat of the discussion is.

The book presents fascinating potential new ways of working through problems, such as the "health avatar" to get a full view of one's health with which the doctors can interact in detail and use statistical methods to better understand the person's health. This can then be even further extended to communities too. It sounds futuristic, but this was proposed in 2009, and we still do not have these proposed methods. The book presents multiple such interesting futuristic proposals.

[^1]: [The Fourth Paradigm: Data-Intensive Scientific Discovery](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/XHD2CC9T?page=1)
