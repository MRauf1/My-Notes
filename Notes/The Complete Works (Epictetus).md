# Review[^1]

## Introduction
The fundamental question he asks is, how must we live our lives to be subjectively fulfilling and objectively worthwhile? The answer is by getting ever close to yourself, your true self.

Some things are not up to us. But our minds are our own, and we can control the inner states. We need to learn to accept the "indifferent" experiences (they are neither good nor bad). These experiences thus can be used as opportunities to practice virtue.

Ultimately, our happiness doesn't depend on external things such as material, our bodies, careers, etc... It depends on our mental faculties and what we make of them.

Similar to certain Hindu philosophy, one should act for the sake of virtue. Any outcome that is separate from virtue is an indifferent, so one should feel neither joy nor sadness at the outcome. One should entirely focus on the virtuous act itself, not the incidental pleasure.

According to Stoics, virtue is knowledge and consists of 4 virtues:
1) Prudential wisdom - knowledge of good and bad.
2) Courage - knowledge of what to fear and what not to fear.
3) Moderation - knowledge of what to pursue and what to avoid.
4) Justice - knowledge of what to give or not give others.

Like Socrates, Stoics believe that humans are inherently good and want what's good for them. Thus, vice is ignorance.

A lot of the Stoic principles are in line with Buddhist teachings too.

3 areas of life where training is needed to make a person free and unconstrained by external hindrances:
1) Desire
2) Inclinations/Action
3) Judgement/Assent

# Handbook

## 1
Up to us are the internal things, such as our judgement, inclination, desire, aversion, but the external things, such as body, wealth, career are not up to us. Focus on what's up to us and don't mix the two categories together, and you'll be free. Judgement is your reasoning/logic applied to thought/assent, while inclination is your reasoning/logic applied to action.

## 2
Desire prevents us from getting what we want and aversion prevents us from avoiding what we want to avoid. As such, withdraw these (for now) from the externals, and only use them to improve our internals, but with a light touch, because even desire or aversion towards what is up to us can end negatively. Ultimately, what truly matters are our judgements and inclinations. Desire and aversion can be used reservedly once we master judgement and inclination.

Stoics recommend withdrawing from desire and aversion for the time being and possibly bringing them back once judgement and inclination are mastered, but Buddhist refrain from desire and aversion altogether, citing the two as the primary causes of suffering.

## 3
When liking something/someone, reduce that thing down to its least reducible and objective state to prevent your own opinions from clouding your judgement as to what that thing truly is.

## 4
In addition to whatever action or activity you're doing, always add in that you want to keep my will in accord with nature in addition to wanting to do the activity. That way, if an activity is interrupted in some way, you can still secure a win by keeping your will in accord with nature.

It may seem contrary to Epictetus telling us to withdraw from desire, but this is the one thing that is appropriate to desire: the integrity of our own will. This is more of a desire of how you want something, rather than what you want. It's geared towards the internal, while the latter is towards the external.

## 5
People suffer not from what is happening, but from their judgements about what is happening. Thus, one has to practice and work on their judgements to make them as reasonable and objective as possible to always seek out the objective truth only.

In the end, things happen in nature, so one should not seek to blame anyone, be it someone else or yourself.

## 6
If you take pride in something, only take pride in the internals, not the externals. And the internal is how you make use of impressions.

## 7
All things that are external are impermanent. As such, do not get attached to any of the externals and be always ready to part away from them when the time comes, even if it's your spouse and children.

## 8
Let go of your desire for wishing things to happen in a specific way, and be content with the natural progression of all things. Detach yourself from the outcome, be it beneficial or harmful to you.

## 9
Externals, such as disease, disability, and such do not affect your will unless you let them. The will is under your power. Your body is separate from your will. No matter what happens, as long as your will is safe and sound, you have not been harmed.

## 10
Always look within yourself at what character qualities or tools you have to deal with each situation before resorting to impressions.

## 11
Nothing you have truly belongs to you as you started off with nothing. Thus, when it comes time for that thing to go, let go and say that you've returned it. Treat all things as belonging to someone else for there is nothing external that truly belongs to you, including your family.

## 12
External things are less scary and frowned upon than internal distresses. It's better to starve with a calm mind than be affluent with a distressed mind.

## 13
Be neutral to both praise and rebuke. Don't be bothered to be thought foolish, nor should you take it to heart if someone things you are an expert. These externalities go against the inner self, and you should prioritize the inner self.

## 14
Confine your efforts in pursuit of things that are within your power. Don't desire to not or avoid things that are up to others.

## 15
Don't desire external things. If it comes to you, modestly take it, but don't hold it back. If it hasn't arrived yet, don't project your desire onto it, but patiently wait your turn. Behave this way to all externalities. If you scorn the external things, even better.

## 16
It's never what happened that affects you (otherwise it would affect others too), but your judgement and opinion of what happened that affects you.

## 17
You're like an actor at stage. You've been assigned a role without your will, but it is up to you to play that role as perfectly as you can.

## 18
Not only do external events not affect you because they do not affect your inner character, but you can use the external events to improve your virtues. In that sense, every omen can be made beneficial to your inner growth.

## 19
Envy and jealousy of others will do you no good. And in the end, all of the external possessions of others that you are jealous of aren't important or valuable because they are external and don't belong to you. Thus, there is nothing to be jealous or envious of others for. You shouldn't even be jealous of other people's inner character or virtues since that would treat virtues as external things, which they are not. Just because someone has some virtue, it doesn't make a difference to whether you can obtain your own virtue for virtues are personal and not a limited resource.

## 20
When, for example, someone is being rude to you, you don't feel insulted because of their rudeness. You feel insulted because of the judgement that they are insulting you. Don't get carried away by this impression and see rudeness or such events for what they are. They don't affect your inner character, so don't let it affect you. Realize this, and you'll be able to control yourself.

## 21
Everyday, imagine all of the terrifying and worst things that can happen happening to you and live through these moments. If you do that consistently and get through them, you'll lessen your desires.

## 22
When mocked or looked down upon or when pursuing a path you deem noble, never act superior to others. Stay true to yourself and follow your virtues. If you keep doing this, even your enemies will come to admire you. On the other hand, by letting these things affect you, you'll end up even more ridiculed.

## 23
If you look outward for approval, then you've lost your bearings. What matters is your inner self and inner thoughts. You don't need anyone but yourself on your path to virtues.

## 24
With respect to others, if you want to help, then help. But only if you can do that while preserving your virtues. If helping others comes with going against your virtues, then you have strayed from the path and will ultimately do no good for both yourself and others.

## 25
Things, especially external, come with a price. If you want them, you have to be willing to pay the price.

## 26
When something bad happens to others, we can remain neutral, but when it happens to us, we feel bad. But we are no different from others in the eyes of nature. When something bad happens to you, maintain the same attitude as when something bad happens to someone else.

## 27
Badness is not a natural feature of the world. Goodness is.

## 28
By letting other people affect your mind, it's as if you're handing your mind directly to them. But the mind belongs to you, so it shouldn't be affected by the externalities.

## 29
With everything, think about what is required and the potential consequences. You cannot focus on both internals and externals. You have to choose one and that will determine what kind of person you will become. A true philosopher would choose the inner self.
Very good passage. Worth rereading for all of the metaphors Epictetus uses to explain this concept.

## 30
Relationships are a good source of stress test to sharpen yourself and find what actions are appropriate for you to take. And no matter what happens, no one can harm you unless you let them. You are only harmed when you believe yourself to be harmed. Just like Buddhists say, pain is inevitable, but suffering is not.

## 31
Goodness and badness do not exist in things that are not up to us. They are only present for things that are up to us.

## 32
Whatever happens in the future, you already know what kind of future it is. It is neither good nor bad. Thus, whatever happens, you can always use the future as an opportunity to sharpen your virtues.

## 33
Set of instructions/advice for how to behave yourself.
Behave yourself the same whether you are by yourself or in the presence of others. Be brief but concise. Be silent until your advice is required or you have something important to say. Don't talk about other people, such as praising others, criticizing others, or comparing others. Whatever the topic of conversation, stay steadfast and remain true to your virtues. Keep things, such as food, in moderation and don't self-indulge. When someone is speaking ill of you, don't worsen the drama. Confine yourself to discussing/talking about mainly about things that are relevant to your moral improvement. Ask yourself what is the worst that can happen, and if you can live through it, then proceed. In social conversations, avoid frequently mentioning about your own life. Don't try to be funny or inappropriate.

## 34
Don't get carried away by impressions of pleasures of external things. Instead, focus on the self-satisfaction and gratification you'll feel having abstained from them.

## 35
Whenever you decide to do something, there is no reason to hide it from others regardless of what other people may say.

## 36
Everything has a benefit and a cost, and everything should be done in moderation. Whenever doing anything, consider all of the possible implications and act with them in mind.

## 37
Take on roles that you are capable of rather than roles that are beyond your capabilities for you will only disgrace yourself in the latter.

## 38
Always protect your thinking mind from outside dangers.

## 39
External things like property and wealth should be used to support the healthy functioning of the internals. In this way, there is always a clear stopping point for how much of the externals you'll use/need. The transgression is going from "Does this fit my need?" to "What else can I add?"

## 40
Focus on the internals rather than the externals, such as beauty.

## 41
Focus on the internals, such as intelligence and thinking, rather than the externals, such as the bodily functions (exercising, drinking, eating, etc...).
